Predicting Scrabble Rating Based on Gameplay¶

Introduction¶

Scrabble is a popular word game played in nearly 121 countries around the world. The primary focus of the game is to create various words with a given set of letters. Players are initially given a certain number of tiles containing letters, and they take turns placing down the letters on the board and creating words. After each turn, players are given points based on the letters which can be multiplied by various point modifiers scattered throughout the board. Players also draw new tiles after each turn to ensure that they have 7 tiles on their rack (the item which holds their tiles). Once all letters have been drawn and a player places their last tile, the game ends. The game can also end if no more plays are possible. More in-depth rules can be found here.

Scrabble was originally a board game; however, its growing popularity and success, brought forth a growing community interested in playing Scrabble online. Woogles.io is a site forged from this very idea. It is a non-profit run by a team of volunteers which are looking to create an online resource of word games, one of which is Scrabble (or OMGwords as it is called on their site).

The project hopes to analyze Scrabble gameplay from Woogles.io and construct a model to predict a player's rating based on the data given. Knowing why certain players have a higher rating can help others improve their own gameplay. Higher rated players do certain things betterthan lower rated players, and by analyzing the differences, lower rated players can study and learn how to become better at the game as well.

Data Processing¶

Let's start by first obtaining and cleaning up our data. Our data is obtained from this Kaggle competition. The data analyzes matches between Scrabble bots, of which there are 3 difficulties, and their human opponents. The goal of the competition is to predict the rating of the human opponent based on the given data.

In the games dataset, there are 12 columns:

  • game_id
  • first (the name of the player that went first)
  • time_control_name (either regular, rapid, blitz, or ultrablitz)
  • game_end_reason (standard, resigned, time, or consecutive zeroes)
  • winner (0 indicates that the first player won, 1 indicates that the second player won, and -1 indicates that the game resulted in a tie)
  • created_at
  • lexicon (the lexicon of the English alphabet that was used for the game)
  • initial_time_seconds (the number of seconds the player start off with)
  • increment_seconds (time increment each player gets each time they play a turn)
  • rating_mode (Whether the game counts towards players' ratings. It is either casual or rated)
  • max_overtime_minutes (an integer between 0 and 10 which represents the maximum number of overtime a player can get)
  • game_duration_seconds

Most of the stuff listed above is self-explanatory but here are some additional details that may be useful:

  • First is either the name of the bot or the nickname of the player based on who went first. There are 3 difficulties of bots: BetterBot (beginner), STEEBot (intermediate), and HastyBot (advanced).
  • If no one scores any points for 6 consecutive turns, the game will end. This is listed as consecutive zeroes in the game_end_reason column.
  • For this data, lexicon is either ECWL, NWL20/NSWL20, or CSW21. In general, there are 2 main authorities for Scrabble word lists: NASPA and Collins. Collins makes the word lists for most countries while NASPA makes the world lists for the US. NASPA lists tend to start with an N such as "NWL" or "NSWL" while Collins lists tend to start with "CSW". The numbers at the end indicate when the list was published.
  • Each overtime minute, or fractional minute, used by the player will result in a loss of 10 points

In the turns dataset, there are 9 columns:

  • game_id
  • turn_number
  • nickname (the name of the player taking the turn)
  • rack (the current tiles in the player's rack. A ? represents a wildcard tile which can be substituted for letter)
  • location (the place at which a move is played)
  • move (the tiles that are placed down in a turn)
  • points (the points that a player gained during that turn)
  • score (the player's total score after that turn)
  • turn_type (the type of that turn)

Most of the stuff listed above is self-explanatory but some of it does need additional explanation. There also are a lot of special scenarios which can change the values in certain columns. I have tried to highlight most of them below.

  • Nickname is either the name of the bot or the nickname of the player based on who went first. There are 3 difficulties of bots as mentioned earlier.
  • A turn in Scrabble can build a word off of existing letters on the board and a "." in the move section is used to indicate that an letter already on the board was used for that turn. A ? in the move column means that a wildcard was exchanged while a lowercase letter in the move column indicates that a wildcard was substituted for that letter

Before moving on, it is important to note the various nuances based on the turn type. Different turn types may result in special characters in certain columns. Here is a description of all of the turn types:

  • Play is the standard turn type where players just play a move.
  • An exchange is where the player exchange some of the tiles on their rack with tiles in the bag (the item which holds all of the undistributed tiles). The move for that turn is marked with a "-" in front such as -ABC to indicate that the player exchanged their A, B, and C tiles. The rack column displays the player's tiles before the exchange. Additionally, an exchange awards no points and the location column shows null for an exchange.
  • A pass indicates that a player simply passed on their turn and played no move. The move column is "-" for this type of move and the location is null.
  • A timeout indicates that a player has ran out of time. The location for that turn is null and the move is (time). The points are negative because 10 points are deducted from a player's score for each minute, or fractional minute, that a player plays exceeding the game's initial time limit.
  • The "End" turn type indicates that a game as ended. In the given data, the only way that a game ends is if one person is completely out of letters to play. The player with no tiles is given the "End" turn and the rack and location of that turn are null. Additionally, the opponent's remaining tiles are scored based on the value of each letter and the player with no tiles is awarded the sum of their opponent's tiles. The move for an end turn is the opponent's rack surrounded by parentheses. For example, if the opponent has A and O left, the move for the "End" turn would be (AO).

A "Challenge" turn type has even more nuances. A challenge occurs when one player questions the validity of another player's word. The word is searched in the appropriate lexicon (as indicated in the games data) and if it exists, the challenge is deemed unsuccessful.

def evaluate(row, type):
  # If human rating is needed, you return the rating the player is a human
  if type == "human":
    if row.nickname not in ["BetterBot", "HastyBot", "STEEBot"]:
      return row.rating
  else:
    if row.nickname in ["BetterBot", "HastyBot", "STEEBot"]:
      return row.rating
  return None;

Hypothesis Testing and Modeling¶

Let's test the relationship between the number of points lost due to challenges and rating.

The null hypothesis is that the rating has no effect on the points lost to challenges. A linear regression model can help prove whether this is true. By inputting the points lost to challenges as the x data and the rating as the y, we can find an R value which will tell us how well a linear regression fits thsoe variables.

In [20]:
from sklearn import linear_model
valid_challenges = human_train_data[pd.notnull(human_train_data["challenges"])]
y_array = [[element] for element in list(valid_challenges["challenges"])]
x_array = [[element] for element in list(valid_challenges["rating"])]
lin_model = linear_model.LinearRegression()
fit_model = lin_model.fit(x_array, y_array)
r_squared = fit_model.score(x_array, y_array)
print("Points lost to Challenges = " 
      + str(fit_model.coef_[0][0]) + " * rating - " + str(fit_model.intercept_[0] * -1))

print("The R^2 Value is " + str(r_squared))

As proven above by the low R value, the relationship between challenges and R value is almost nonexistent. The R value should be close to 1; however, it is of magnitude 10^-5 which is very far from 1.

Based on our graphs and analysis so far, only score and exchanges per game seems to have an impact on a human player's rating. Conclusion¶

Overall, there seem to be few things in the dataset which directly correlate with a higher player rating. Only exchanges and score seemed to display a correlation when they were graphed. Not many insights were made and few things were learned. There are a few possible avenues which could be pursued in the future

  • Analyzing the length of each move and how that could correlate to rating
  • Looking at positional trends and analyzing if higher rated players play their move more strategically on the board than lower rated players. Since the board contains squares which grant extra points this could result in a trend.
